Passwords Are Broken (Here's What Actually Works)

Most password advice is wrong. Most password policies make things worse. Here's what the research says, what's realistic, and how to pick a strategy that actually fits your life.

~12 min read 5 readings 1 podcast
Core Question

Why do the most common password policies — the ones your IT department probably enforces — actually make you less secure? And what should you realistically do about it?

Let's Be Honest About Passwords

You have too many of them. You reuse some of them. You know you shouldn't. You've been told to use a password manager and you might have one but you don't always use it. When your hospital forces you to change your password every 90 days, you add a ! to the end and call it a day.

You're not alone. The numbers are staggering:

  • Researchers analyzed 19 billion leaked passwords and found that only 6% were unique. The other 94% were reused or weak.
  • 78% of people globally admit to reusing passwords across sites.
  • Compromised credentials are involved in nearly half of all data breaches.
  • Healthcare has the highest average data breach cost of any sector: approximately $9 million per incident.
  • Nurses spend an average of 45 minutes per shift just logging into systems.

The gap between "what you should do" and "what's realistic" is enormous. Most security advice ignores this. We won't.

Why Most Password Policies Make Things Worse

You've seen the rules: "Must contain at least one uppercase letter, one lowercase letter, one number, and one special character. Must be changed every 90 days. Cannot reuse your last 12 passwords."

These policies feel secure. Here's what actually happens:

  • Complexity rules create predictable patterns. When forced to include a number and special character, most people do the same thing: Password1! satisfies every complexity rule. It's cracked in under a second.
  • Forced rotation causes iteration. Summer2025! → Fall2025! → Winter2026!. Attackers know this pattern because they've seen it millions of times in breach data.
  • Frustration breeds workarounds. Passwords taped to monitors. Shared logins at nursing stations. "Everyone knows the Wi-Fi password is taped inside the supply closet." The more painful the policy, the more creative the circumvention.
"NIST no longer recommends periodic password changes. Users tend to choose weaker passwords when they know they'll have to change them." — NIST SP 800-63B, Digital Identity Guidelines
What NIST Actually Recommends Now

The National Institute of Standards and Technology — the gold standard for security guidelines — updated their password recommendations in 2024:

  • DO require a minimum of 15 characters (they allow up to 64)
  • DO check passwords against known breach lists
  • DO allow all printable characters including spaces
  • DON'T require specific character types (uppercase, numbers, symbols)
  • DON'T require periodic password changes unless there's evidence of compromise
  • DON'T use password hints or security questions

Most healthcare organizations are still enforcing policies NIST explicitly recommends against. If you're in a position to change policy, point your auditor to NIST SP 800-63B.

Outdated Policy

  • Minimum 8 characters
  • Must include upper, lower, number, symbol
  • Change every 90 days
  • Can't reuse last 12 passwords
  • Security questions for recovery

What really happens: Spring2026! on a sticky note under the keyboard.

NIST-Aligned Policy

  • Minimum 15 characters
  • No composition rules
  • No forced rotation
  • Checked against breach databases
  • MFA required

What really happens: marble-canopy-thunder-bicycle in a password manager, left alone.

Six Ways to Create Passwords (Honestly Reviewed)

There's no single right answer. Different approaches work for different people and different situations. Here are six real options — with honest assessments of each.

1. The Diceware Method (Random Word Passphrases)

Roll physical dice (or use a generator) to pick 5-6 random words from a curated word list. The original Diceware method uses actual dice for true randomness. The EFF published an improved word list with more memorable words.

Example: marble canopy thunder bicycle frost

Strength: Excellent. 5 Diceware words ≈ 64 bits of entropy. 6 words ≈ 77 bits. Effectively uncrackable by brute force.

The catch: You have to actually use random words, not words you chose. "I love my golden retriever" feels like a passphrase but has far less entropy because humans are predictable. Roll the dice or use a generator.

Best for: Your master password. The one password you actually memorize.

2. The XKCD Method (Simplified Diceware)

Inspired by the famous XKCD comic #936, this is essentially Diceware for people who don't want to roll dice. Use a generator like useapassphrase.com to get random words.

Example: correct horse battery staple

Strength: Good with 4+ truly random words. The comic's original 4-word example is now considered the minimum — go with 5-6 for AI tool accounts.

The catch: The comic is from 2011. Attackers now include passphrase patterns in their dictionaries. Still strong if the words are random — weak if you pick your own.

Best for: People who want something memorable without installing anything.

3. Let the Password Manager Generate It

Open 1Password, Bitwarden, or your browser's password manager. Click "Generate." Accept whatever 20+ character random string it creates. Never look at it again.

Example: kW9#mP2$vL5nR8@xQ4

Strength: Maximum. You can't get more random than a cryptographic generator.

The catch: You can't type it from memory. You're fully dependent on the manager. If you lose access to your vault and don't have your recovery key, you're locked out of everything. This is the single biggest fear that stops people from adopting password managers — and it's not irrational.

Best for: Every account except your master password and your email recovery.

4. The Sentence Method

Take a sentence only you would know, and use the first letters of each word plus some numbers from your life that aren't obvious.

Example: "My first apartment was on 4th and Oak in 2012" → Mfawo4&Oi2012

Strength: Moderate. Better than a dictionary word, but shorter than passphrases and still somewhat guessable if someone knows your history.

The catch: People overestimate how unique their sentences are. "I graduated from [school] in [year]" is less unique than you think. And you'll forget which sentence you used for which account within a month.

Best for: Situations where you can't use a password manager (shared workstations, kiosk systems).

5. Your Browser's Built-In Manager

Chrome, Safari, and Firefox all generate and save strong passwords. They sync across your devices. They autofill. They're already installed.

Strength: Good. The generated passwords are cryptographically random and unique per site.

The catch: They're tied to your browser ecosystem. Chrome passwords don't easily move to Safari. If you use multiple browsers, things get messy. Sharing passwords with a spouse or team member is harder. And if your Google/Apple/Firefox account is compromised, so are all your passwords.

Best for: People who won't use a dedicated manager (and that's OK). A browser manager with unique passwords for every site is dramatically better than reusing passwords — don't let perfect be the enemy of good.

6. Passkeys (The Future, Available Now)

Passkeys replace passwords entirely. You authenticate with your fingerprint, face, or device PIN. They're unique per site, can't be phished, can't be reused, and can't be guessed. Google, Apple, Microsoft, 1Password, and Bitwarden all support them.

Strength: Maximum. Phishing-resistant by design.

The catch: Not every site supports them yet. Most AI tools don't yet. When they do — use them. In the meantime, you still need passwords for everything else.

Best for: Every site that supports them. Enable passkeys wherever you see the option.

The Healthcare Password Reality

Let's talk about what actually happens in clinics and hospitals, because it's different from what happens in a security textbook.

What We Know

Nurses spend an average of 45 minutes per shift just logging into systems. Shared workstations require constant log-in/log-out cycles. Passwords get taped to monitors, shared between shifts, and left on sticky notes in supply closets. This isn't laziness — it's a system designed without empathy for the people who use it.

The honest answer to "how do we fix passwords in healthcare" involves acknowledging some uncomfortable truths:

  • Shared workstations are a reality. Not everyone has a personal laptop. The nursing station computer gets used by 15 different people per shift. Badge-tap SSO and proximity-based login exist, but many facilities don't have them. Until they do, the best you can do is log out when you walk away and use a passphrase you can actually type quickly.
  • Password managers don't work on every system. Your EHR login, your Pyxis station, your paging system — many clinical systems don't support browser extensions or autofill. For those, you need a password you can type from memory. The sentence method or a short passphrase is the realistic answer.
  • Perfect compliance isn't the goal. Moving from "same password everywhere" to "three tiers of passwords" (one for critical systems, one for work tools, one for low-stakes accounts) is a real improvement, even if it's not perfect. Progress beats perfection.
  • 65% of people don't trust password managers. That's not ignorance — it's a reasonable concern about putting all eggs in one basket. The answer isn't to dismiss the concern; it's to address it. (Recovery keys. Export your vault. Don't depend on a single cloud provider.)
"Password sharing is only a symptom of systemic issues with healthcare workflows." — Telmediq

Pick Your Level (Be Honest About Where You Are)

Starting Out
Stop reusing passwords for AI tools. Let your browser generate and save unique ones. Enable MFA everywhere.
Getting Serious
Set up a real password manager. Create a Diceware master password. Import your browser passwords. Enable breach monitoring.
Locked Down
Hardware security keys. Passkeys everywhere supported. Separate vaults for work and personal. Regular breach checks.

The Absolute Minimum (Do These Today)

  1. Check if you've been breached: Go to haveibeenpwned.com. Enter your email. If anything shows up, change those passwords now.
  2. Enable MFA on your AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini). This takes 2 minutes per account. Even if your password is terrible, MFA stops most attacks.
  3. Stop reusing your email password. Your email account is the skeleton key — it's the recovery method for everything else. Give it a unique passphrase. Memorize it. Write it down and store it somewhere safe (yes, writing it down is fine if it's in a drawer at home, not on your monitor).
Yes, Writing Down Passwords Can Be OK

Security researcher Bruce Schneier has long advocated that writing passwords on paper and keeping them in your wallet is reasonable for most threat models. Your threat isn't someone breaking into your house — it's automated credential stuffing attacks from breached databases. A unique password on a piece of paper in a drawer defeats that attack completely. A reused password memorized perfectly does not.

The Bottom Line

The best password system is the one you'll actually use. A browser password manager with unique passwords for every site is dramatically better than reusing the same password everywhere. A dedicated manager is better still. Passkeys are the future.

But the single most important thing you can do today isn't about passwords at all — it's enabling MFA. A bad password with MFA enabled is more secure than a perfect password without it.
