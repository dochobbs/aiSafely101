Shadow AI — The Risk You Can't See

Your team is almost certainly already using AI tools you don't know about.

~12 min read 5 readings 2 podcasts

We Get It — These Tools Are Incredible

Let's start with something important: if you've been using AI tools to draft notes, summarize articles, brainstorm treatment approaches, or get through your inbox faster — that makes sense. These tools are genuinely useful. A physician who uses Claude to help compose a thoughtful response to a patient, an MA who uses ChatGPT to decode an insurance denial, a billing specialist who uses AI to catch coding errors — these are people trying to do better work.

The problem isn't the impulse. The problem is that many organizations haven't provided approved tools — or even clear guidance — leaving their teams to figure it out alone. When there's no sanctioned option, people find unsanctioned ones. That's human nature, not malice.

This Isn't About Blame

If you've been using a consumer AI tool at work because your organization hasn't provided an alternative — you're not alone, and this guide isn't here to make you feel bad. It's here to help you understand the risks so you can make informed choices and, ideally, advocate for your organization to provide safe, approved tools.

The Data on Shadow AI

The Weekend Test

A 2025 study by Gener8 found that GenAI usage drops 13% on weekends, with total visits falling 23%. Adults aged 25–34 see a 28% weekend decline.

People are using these tools primarily at work — and many of them are using consumer tools without their organization's knowledge or approval.

Shadow AI usage has surged over 200% in high-stakes sectors including healthcare, manufacturing, and financial services. According to IBM's 2025 Cost of Data Breach Report, AI-associated breaches cost organizations more than $650,000 per incident. In one documented case, developers at a healthcare startup were found using ChatGPT to debug code containing patient data examples — a potential $3.2 million fine.

Of ChatGPT's 800+ million regular users, 1 in 4 submits a healthcare-related prompt every week. About 40 million people use it daily for health questions. More than 60% of workers rely on personal, unmanaged AI tools rather than enterprise-approved ones — yet when organizations provide approved AI tools, unauthorized use drops 89%.

The scale of AI use in healthcare is already massive. The question isn't whether your team is using AI — it's whether they're using it safely.

Approved vs. Unapproved Tools

Every organization should maintain a clear list of approved AI tools. If yours doesn't have one, that's a red flag — not permission to use whatever you want.

Approved
Organization has vetted the tool, signed a BAA if needed, and trained staff
Conditionally Approved
OK for non-sensitive work only (marketing copy, not patient communications)
Unapproved
No vetting, no BAA, not permitted for any work involving sensitive data
Ask Before You Paste

Before putting any information into any AI tool, ask yourself: "Would I be comfortable if this appeared on a billboard?" If the answer is no, verify the tool is approved and covered by a BAA before proceeding.

What You Can Do Right Now

Whether your organization has an approved tool list or not, here are concrete steps you can take today:

  • Ask your IT team or practice manager: "What AI tools are we allowed to use?" If they don't have an answer, you've just identified a gap that needs filling.
  • If you've been using consumer AI tools at work: Stop using them with any patient data or sensitive information. You can still use them for non-sensitive tasks (drafting marketing copy, researching general medical topics, writing policies).
  • Advocate for approved alternatives: Share this guide with your compliance officer or practice manager. The tools exist — Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google all offer HIPAA-compliant tiers. The barrier is usually awareness, not cost.
  • Don't wait for a perfect solution: If your organization can't immediately deploy enterprise AI, at least establish clear rules about what's off-limits (patient data, in all forms) and what's OK (general knowledge tasks with no identifiers).

What Shadow AI Looks Like in Practice

Shadow AI isn't malicious — it's people trying to be productive. Common examples:

  • A front desk coordinator pastes an insurance denial letter into ChatGPT to draft an appeal — with the patient's name and diagnosis still in it.
  • A physician takes a photo of a complex lab result and uploads it to Claude for interpretation — the image contains the patient's full identifiers.
  • A billing specialist uses an AI browser extension to auto-summarize claim notes — the extension reads every page they visit, including the EHR.
  • A developer asks an AI coding assistant to help with a database query — the example data includes real patient records.

None of these people intended to cause a breach. All of them created one.

"When approved tools are provided, unauthorized use drops 89%."
The Bottom Line

Shadow AI isn't a people problem — it's a policy problem. Prohibition doesn't work; people will find tools that make them productive. The answer is to provide approved alternatives and make the safe path the easy path. If your organization doesn't have an approved AI tool list, advocate for one. If it does, follow it.
